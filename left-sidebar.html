Tasmanian Eucalyptus

Tasmania, Australia hosts 29 native species of Eucalyptus from two major subgenera: Eucalyptus and Symphyomyrtus. A few notable species include Eucalyptus globulus (timber), Eucalyptus regnans (tallest tree), and Eucalyptus radiata (essential oil).

An evolutionary case for plant rarity: Eucalyptus as a model system

Ecology & Evolution

Rarity as an evolved syndrome of traits.

  • Rare species have lower biomass and allocate more resources aboveground than common species
  • Plant biomass is under evolutionary selection: diverging selection between rare and common species, but convergent selection across the phylogeny
  • Plant biomass, under selection, is highly correlated with species' range size and habitat specificity

Functional traits under evolutionary selection drive geographic patterns in plant occurrence and therefore rarity.

Evolution of rarity and phylogeny determine above-and belowground biomass in plant-plant interactions

PLOS ONE

Rare species may use facilitative interactions with species based on rarity and phylogenetic relatedness to persist in complex communities, but in low frequency.

  • Rare species demonstrate progressively positive non-additive responses in biomass when interacting with phylogenetically intermediate, less rare species.
  • Rare species benefit from interactions with specific species, such as Eucalyptus globulus.

Eucalyptus Research

Tasmanian Eucalyptus as a model system to study the evolution of plant rarity and the role of rarity in species-species interactions.
